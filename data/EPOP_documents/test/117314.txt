23% more citrus interceptions from third countries
According to data collected by the Spanish producer association La Unió Llauradora, the number of citrus interceptions from third countries due to the presence of pests has increased by 23%… According to data collected by the Spanish producer association La Unió Llauradora, the number of citrus interceptions from third countries due to the presence of pests has increased by 23% compared to last year (January-September data). "The Mercosur countries Brazil, Argentina and Uruguay represent 60% of the total interceptions in the first nine months of this year. Brazil has 63 interceptions, which is 530% more than last year's 10. Argentina recorded 8 (3 more than last year) and Uruguay 4 (last year the country had a flawless report). Of the countries associated with Mercosur, Colombia has 3 interceptions, compared to 1 last year." "For South Africa, the number of interceptions has decreased. It should be noted that it only started to export strongly in September and that it has stopped orange exports from the areas with pest pressure. Despite the suspension of these shipments, there are There have been 16 interceptions this year, including 4 in September, 13 of these interceptions are attributed to Phyllosticta citricarpa, a fungus that causes the dreaded Citrus Black Spot." Because of the danger posed by these pests, La Unió is requesting the automatic suspension of imports from countries that cannot guarantee the phytosanitary safety of their exports. The organization also calls for the implementation of a uniform cold treatment system for all imports and for the EU to use one standard criterion for all countries. Incidentally, La Unió considers ratifying or signing agreements between the EU with trading blocs such as Mercosur or South Africa a serious mistake, "since there are no in-depth or rigorous impact studies on the impact of imports on our horticultural sector or on the consequences of a possible introduction of a plague, something we are already paying the price for with Xylella or with the cotonet from South Africa". "All agreements without reciprocity in cultivation standards or in the use of active substances, without full phytosanitary guarantees in terms of pests, without the obligations established in the 'Farm to Fork' strategy and without proper organization as to the import periods concerns - to avoid overlap with the European offer - lead to the mistake we have already made several times, which has a huge negative impact on the European sector," says Carles Peris, general secretary of La Unió. "The introduction of a new pest or disease would increase crop protection costs even more and would also damage the role of citrus as a fruit for the fresh market", concludes Peris.
