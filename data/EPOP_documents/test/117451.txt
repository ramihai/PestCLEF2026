BeXyl is the name of the new major European research project on the Xylella fastidiosa, which has an investment of 7 million euros from the Horizon Europe program, an expected duration of four years and will be coordinated by Blanca B. Landa, a researcher at the Institute of Sustainable Agriculture (IAS-CSIC). Although it has 31 members from fourteen different countries (France, Italy, Slovenia, Austria, Belgium, Germany, the United Kingdom, Israel, Argentina, Brazil, the United States, Costa Rica and Australia), Spain has a prominent role in this ambitious project, which picks up the baton from the two previous large projects financed by the European Union: Ponte and XF-Actors. Thus, from Spain, in addition to the IAS-CS, the universities of Córdoba and Girona, IFAPA, IVIA, IRFAP of the Balearic Islands and the companies Soluciones Energéticas Levante and Agromillora collaborate. Active participation is also expected from more than forty government agencies, nurseries and farmers' associations, NGOs and various EU operational groups. The goal is to create a community of scientists, farmers, and policy makers to bridge the gap between research and field applications. Beyond Xylella, Integrated Management Strategies for Mitigating Xylella fastidiosa impact in Europe Xylella fastidiosa in Europe), has been conceived to strengthen prevention and improve control strategies for the disease, the most dangerous in the European Union due to its economic, social and environmental impact (it leads thirteen of the 25 indicators used to identify the twenty priority pests and diseases in the EU). BeXyl will establish a global network of 'sentinel plots' that provide data from a wide range of latitudes and conditions. The project will support studies on the drivers of Xylella fastidiosa epidemics in the EU and will improve detection methods for the bacterium and its vectors, with tools ranging from remote sensing to the sense of smell of dogs. Other lines of research include heat treatment to ensure the safe exchange of plant material, genetic traits for plant resistance, bacteria capable of reducing the spread of bacteria within plants, and innovative solutions to help farmers use tools. methods to control the vectors of the bacteria. The University of Córdoba hosted the first meeting of the participants in this project from October 17 to 19. This week, Blanca B. Landa presented BeXyl at the XX National Congress of the Spanish Phytopathology Society, of which she has just been elected president. There, he stressed the importance of detecting the appearance of a new outbreak in time, and the crucial role that remote sensing, with the use of high-resolution hyperspectral and thermal sensors, can play in this regard. He also explained that, in light of the complex genetic diversity of the strains identified in Europe, it is advisable to manage the outbreaks with an ad hoc strategy designed for each particular case. Another line of research developed by the IAS-CSIC starts from the study of the microbiome that inhabits the xylem of the plant and in the soil to combat the pathogen with microbial consortia artificially inoculated in the tree.
