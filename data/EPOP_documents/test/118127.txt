The African HLB vector spreads through Portugal and threatens citrus plantations in Huelva
The CGC warns that the medium-term future of citrus farming is threatened, which is why it urges the EC to tighten its controls on imports The CGC warns that the medium-term future of citrus farming is threatened, which is why it urges the EC to tighten its controls on imports Valencia Fruits. Writing. In October 2021, the first detection was made in the Portuguese citrus region of the Algarve, in Aljezur. One year later, the Trioza erytreae has colonized some 200 kilometers of the southernmost Portuguese Atlantic coast and is now less than 120 km from the first plantations in Huelva, in Ayamonte. Since it was detected in Porto in 2015, the feared psyllid has expanded without interruption along the entire coast of the neighboring country and has also colonized part of Galicia (it was detected earlier, in 2014), Cantabria, Asturias and the Basque Country. The unstoppable spread of the insect that carries the African strain of HLB or Huanglongbing -which is the least virulent but equally harmful- was already confirmed by the latest audit of the European Commission (EC) carried out on Portugal in November last year. In that report, beyond reassuring by ruling out the presence of the bacteria that causes the disease, it already warned that the measures implemented by the neighboring country "are insufficient to eradicate, or even to contain" the Trioza. This fact, added to the confirmation in January of the presence of the other vector of this disease in Israel – the Diaphorina citri, which has a greater capacity to adapt to Mediterranean climates and transmits the more aggressive Asian variety and the African variety "question the medium-term future of Spanish citrus farming," warns the president of the Citrus Management Committee (CGC), Inmaculada Sanfeliu. Knowing that the T. erytreae has settled along the entire Algarve coastline, which is the country's main citrus-growing area -with some 16,000 hectares and 370,000 tons of production- but also a tourist region with a landscape where scattered residences with small orchards abound /gardens, it is not difficult to intuit that the insect is advancing now –as it already did further north in Portugal- inland, towards Huelva. The distance from the first Mediterranean focus of D. citri found in Israel makes it no less of a threat. And this is so because, as with the other vector, the main route of entry would be the importation of fruits from affected areas as well as contaminated plant material (plants or twigs for grafting that are not subject to quarantine). All in all, the recent verification of its presence in the Hefer Valley, in a coastal city north of Tel Aviv, proves its greater capacity for adaptation –against T.erytreae– to the Mediterranean climate, to warmer temperatures. In addition, research from the Valencian Institute of Agrarian Research (IVIA)-Jaume I University and the University of Florida have proven that the pattern on which the vast majority of orange and mandarin trees in Spain is based – the Citrange reed– “is an extremely favorable host for the development and reproduction” of the so-called Asian psyllid. Both in Israel now, as well as in Florida and even more so in Brazil, the latter places devastated by HLB and where this vector serves to spread the disease, the fight against the insect is based on the constant use of phytosanitary treatments with products that, in the vast majority are banned in the EU and at least serve to mitigate or slow down the damage caused by the Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus bacterium. The Hebrew country officially reported, in January 2022, the presence of D.citri six months after confirming it (in July), since then there has been no official information about it. the evolution of this pest. Spanish citrus production would be reduced by half in 7 years and could disappear in less than 15 if the HLB bacterium finally arrived According to a study carried out by the CGC itself, which extrapolated the data on the advance of this disease in Florida, the Spanish citrus industry would be reduced by half in 7 years and could disappear in less than 15 if the HLB bacterium finally arrived . Hence, the association now insists on the need for the EC to strengthen border controls to the extreme -both with the port inspection of fruits and that of passengers, who can also carry infected plant material- and demands the urgent review of the Community strategy on synthetic products which, according to the latest proposal for a regulation on the sustainable use of phytosanitary products, would imply their prohibition in large areas devoted to citrus production. “Biological control, as proven by the results obtained by the parasitoid Tamarixia dryi in the Canary Islands or Galicia, works and obtains results against Trioza but, as the situation in Portugal shows –where it has also been released-, it is not enough. Faced with the worst disease, we need to have all the weapons available”, remarks the president of the CGC, Inmaculada Sanfeliu. Among them, the CGC has deposited well-founded hopes in the line of investigation opened at the IVIA. Indeed, the Moncada center –responsible for importing the aforementioned parasitoid from South Africa- works hand in hand with the Institute of Plant Molecular and Cellular Biology (IBMCP) in obtaining peptides with antibacterial activity for the control of HLB; in the genetic improvement of citrus varieties with resistance to Candidatus Liberibacter asiaticus and in the editing of citrus protoplasts using CRISPR/Cas-9. Paradoxically, such a revolutionary editing technique and contrary to the rest of the planet, continues to be regulated in the EU by the same restrictive regulations as transgenics. The CGC –like practically the entire scientific community- calls for this regulatory framework to be urgently reviewed as well. Otherwise, the varieties or rootstocks that could be obtained through this technology -in Europe or in the rest of the world- resistant or tolerant to HLB, salinity, greater water stress... could not be cultivated in the old continent.
