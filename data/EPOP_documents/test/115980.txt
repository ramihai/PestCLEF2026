Xylella: an electronic device against the bacterium | OK Cheers
Finding out plants infected with Xylella: a system for intervening on Xylella directly in the field before its damage occurs. An electronic biosensor for finding plants infected with Xylella, capable of quickly and accurately identifying the presence of a single bacterium directly on the plant. This device could revolutionize the real battle of olive growers against Xylella fastidiosa. The result of the research was published in the scientific journal . Advanced Science Researchers from the University of Bari and the Cnr, in collaboration with Agritest S.r.l. they wanted to find a method to hunt down this bacterium as quickly as possible, even before its effects on plants are visible. At the moment, to find plants infected with Xylella, suitable personnel and a laboratory are needed Up to now, as Professor Luisa Torsi who coordinated the study explains, we have used molecular detection tests, which use the DNA of the bacterium itself as a target. “These analyses, however, require the use of laboratory infrastructures, together with analysis times of at least 3 hours. The innovative electronic platform proposed by the team of researchers from Bari would instead allow the detection of the single bacterium in just 30 minutes directly in the field". There is no cure yet to restore affected plants “At the moment, the early detection of the bacterium and the containment measures implemented in the regional territories seem to have slowed down the spread of the bacterium. We need fast and reliable tools to diagnose and intervene early trying to stop the disease. At the moment, experts have not yet found treatments capable of healing the infected plants". Donato Boscia is responsible for the Bari office of the Institute for the Sustainable Protection of Plants (Cnr-Ipsp). He is among the pioneers in the fight against Xylella fastidiosa in Puglia. Finding out plants infected with Xylella: it could change the fight against this bacterium Not having to wait for laboratory tests and understand if the bacterium is present before the effects are seen, can change the fight against this bacterium that has brought many farmers to their knees. The main limitations of the 'classical' laboratory methods concern: the need to resort to specialized personnel, the use of complex instruments. “Biosensors based on single-molecule transistor (SiMoT) technology have a concrete prospect of being used for diagnostics in the field of plant weed diseases”. Eleonora Macchia is a researcher at the Department of Pharmacy-Drug Sciences of the University of Bari, winner of an ERC Starting Grant project.
