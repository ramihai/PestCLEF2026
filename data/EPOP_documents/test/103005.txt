“Plants in danger”: a national awareness campaign for plant health Launched by the Ministry of Agriculture and Food Sovereignty in July 2022, the national campaign "Plants in danger" aims to prevent the spread of organisms harmful to plants, bacteria, viruses or insects carried by commercial flows. plants or by travelers who bring them back from their travels. Plants in danger: all concerned Aimed at the general public, private individuals and amateur gardeners, the challenge of this campaign is to encourage as many people as possible to identify the symptoms and report any contamination to the services in charge of the health of the plants present in each region ( SRAL/DRAAF). As key players in the prevention of the disease, it is essential to make them aware of the risks represented by these harmful organisms and of the need to only buy duly controlled plants with a plant passport. This is why this campaign is also aimed at plant trade professionals and local authorities. Among the organisms that can cause damage to plants, the bacterium Xylella fastidiosa , the Japanese beetle and Asian beetles are put forward to improve surveillance on French territory. The Japanese beetle ( Popillia japonica) Popillia japonica) Native to Japan, it is a polyphagous insect that attacks a large number of plants, wild and cultivated (including 404 host plants of 92 botanical families) by consuming the foliage. Its larvae cause damage to roots, which are particularly visible on grassy surfaces (grass meadows, lawns, golf courses, etc.) due to plant dieback. In continental Europe, the first reports were made in Italy in 2014, then in Switzerland from 2017. In 2021, the species was also detected near the French border, in Basel (Switzerland) as part of the monitoring of organisms harmful to plants. Asian capricorns ( Anoplophora glabripennis and A. chinensis ) Anoplophora glabripennis and A. chinensis ) Native to eastern Asia, the Asian longhorn beetle ( Anoplophora glabripennis ) and the Asian citrus longhorn beetle ( Anoplophora chinensis ) pose threats to many wild and cultivated hardwood species. The larvae devour the tissues and damage the conductive vessels, thus interrupting the circulation of the sap. Causing the dieback of host plants, the presence of these beetles can go so far as to cause the death of the tree. In France, a first focus of Asian Capricorn was detected in 2003 in Loiret. Since then, four other outbreaks of Asian capricorn have been declared in different regions. Three have been successfully eradicated, in Loire-Atlantique (2010), in Bas-Rhin (2019) and in Haute-Corse (2022), and two are the subject of containment measures. The Asian citrus capricorn is much less common. Its first colonization in Europe was discovered in Italy in 2000 and a new contamination, now considered eradicated, followed in France in 2003. Xylella fastidiosa Xylella fastidiosa The bacterium Xylella fastidiosa is transmitted by insects and can attack more than 360 identified host species, including vines, olive trees, fruit trees, citrus trees, coffee trees, oaks, etc. To date, no treatment can prevent the dieback of infected plants. This xylem bacterium prevents the plant from feeding by interfering with the circulation of raw sap. The associated symptoms are not very specific (wilting, leaf burns) and make it difficult to detect. Detected for the first time in France, in October 2015, on myrtle-leaved Milkwort ( Polygala myrtifolia ) plants in South Corsica and in the Provence-Alpes Côte d'Azur region. In 2020, it was detected in Occitania. These three harmful organisms are priority quarantine organisms, according to European Regulation 2016/2031/EU: their potential economic, environmental or social impact is considered to be the most serious for the territory of the European Union. Regulations relating to quarantine organisms Phytosanitary regulations were gradually put in place at the international level after the serious decline of European vines in the 1870s due to an exotic insect of American origin responsible for Phylloxera, to lead to the Adoption in 1952 of the International Plant Protection Convention. The main tasks of national plant protection organizations are to prevent the introduction and establishment of plant pests and diseases of exotic origin. These organisms are referred to as quarantine pests. They are subject to control when plants are imported and to territorial surveillance and compulsory control measures in the event of detection. A campaign to raise public awareness As part of the “Plants in danger” campaign, several communication media have been made available by the ministry. Posters, videos, banners and materials for social networks as well as radio spots can be downloaded from the Communication Kit. Reporting your observations with participatory science The AGIIR application (Alert & Manage Invasive Insects and/or Pests), developed by INRAe, makes it possible to create a network of geolocated sentinels, capable of monitoring crops and reporting harmful and invasive organisms in France. Recognition sheets for seven species are available on the application and the website to confirm the identification of these insects, and communicate information on their biology and the strategies allowing their reasoned and eco-responsible management: pine processionary, Thaumetopoea pitycampa oak processionary, Thaumetopoea processionea box tree moth, Cydalima perspectalis palmivorous butterfly, Paysandisia archon Asian Hornet, Vespa velutina evil bug, Halyomorpha halys Japanese Beetle, Popillia japonica Once the insect has been identified, the observations can be transmitted by taking care to georeference the place and by completing a short questionnaire directly on the smartphone application. It is also possible to declare the presence of one of these pests from your internet browser, by registering on the ephytia site. Thanks to the AGIIR application, the progression of the stink bug ( Halyomorpha halys ) could be certified and made it possible to inform the services of the ministry on the evolution of this pest.
