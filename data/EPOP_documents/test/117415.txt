International: Scientists have refuted the powerlessness of fungicides against the terrible Panama banana disease In fact, you can save banana plantations with a well-known fungicide. A team of researchers (Stuart Cannon, William Kay, Sridhar Kilaru, Martin Schuster, Sarah Jane Gurr, Gero Steinberg) published in an article in journals.plos.org the results of comparative experiments of pesticides against pathogens of new strains of Fusarium wilt of bananas threatening to destroy a popular global product with a production of 114 million tons per year. “The global supply of bananas has previously been threatened by Fusarium oxysporum f. sp. cubense (Foc) because in the 1950s Race 1 (FocR1) wiped out the commercially dominant variety Gros Michel. This problem was overcome with the introduction of the resistant cultivar Cavendish, the authors write. – However, with the advent of the Tropical Race 4 (FocTR4) strain, discovered in Taiwan in 1967, the global supply of bananas has once again become a concern, as an aggressive new strain dubbed “Panama disease” has established itself. spread across the continents, reaching the South. America in the year 2019. The Panama disease threat is of great importance as Cavendish bananas currently account for about 40% of world production and more than 90% of all exports. FocTR4 (also called Fusarium odoratissimum ) produces 3 forms of asexual spores: macroconidia, microconidia, and chlamydospores. Studies of the subtropical race Foc 4 suggest that these spore forms are involved in the infection of bananas. Melanized thick-walled chlamydospores survive harsh environmental conditions and persist in soil debris for decades, dispersed by nonhost species or carried on soil, shoes, or farm equipment. Chlamydospores germinate under favorable conditions, penetrate root tissues, invade the cortex, and enter the endoderm, where they colonize and block xylem vessels. The infection causes wilting, yellowing of the leaves, darkening of the blood vessels, necrosis and death of the plants. It has previously been suggested that there are no effective fungicide-based strategies to control this soil-borne pathogen. Studies on the effects of various fungicides on FocTR4 in vitro or in vivo, by corm injection, exposed root dipping, and/or soil soaking, have yielded conflicting results. Thus, "chemical measures are considered to be of limited or questionable efficacy" or "fusarium wilt cannot be controlled with fungicides." Here, we address these claims and perform robust quantitative analyzes to evaluate 21 fungicides from 7 classes for (i) FocTR4 and FocR1 colony formation in vitro and, for comparison, fungicide-sensitive wheat pathogen Zymoseptoria tritici , (ii) survival of macrospores FocTR4 microspores, chlamydospores in liquid culture. This systematic approach allowed us to (i) describe the likely reasons for the failure of major classes of fungicides to control FocTR4 (ii) identify a fungicide that actually works. We tested the effect of 12 individual (single target) and 9 multisite fungicides against FocTR4 and Foc Race1 (FocR1) on quantitative colony growth and on FocTR4 macroconidia, microconidia, and chlamydospores. All of these FocTR4 morphotypes cause Panama disease in bananas. These experiments revealed resistance of FocTR4 to all single-acting fungicides, whereas neither azoles, succinate dehydrogenase inhibitors (SDHI), strobilurins, or benzimidazoles kill these spore forms. We have shown in fungicide-treated hyphae that this resistance occurs in a subpopulation of "persistent" cells and is not genetically inherited. FocTR4 persisters respond to 3 µg/mL azoles or 1000 µg/mL strobilurins or SDHIs by potent upregulation of genes encoding target enzymes (up to 660-fold), putative effluent transporter genes (up to 230-fold), and xenobiotic detoxification enzymes (up to 200 at one time). Comparison of gene expression in FocTR4 and Zymoseptoria tritici grown under identical conditions shows that this response is only seen in FocTR4. In contrast, FocTR4 exhibits little inherent resistance to most fungicides that act at multiple target sites. However, quantitative analyzes show that only captan (20 µg/ml) and all lipophilic cations (200 µg/ml) effectively suppress Panama disease. However, this is good news, believing that pesticides have no power over FocTR4.”
