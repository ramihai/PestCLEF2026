Interceptions of citrus fruits from countries THE UNION requests the automatic suspension of imports from those third countries that cannot guarantee the phytosanitary safety of their shipments The data are once again scandalous and highlight the latent risk of a lethal pest entering the citrus trees of the European Union. Interceptions of citrus fruits from third countries with pests have increased by 23% up to September compared to last year, according to information gathered by LA UNIÓ Llauradora. Mercosur countries (Brazil, Argentina and Uruguay) account for 60% of all interceptions in the first nine months of the year. Brazil goes from the 10 accumulated in 2021 for these dates to the 63 of this one (+530%), Argentina from 5 to 8 (+200%) and Uruguay 4 after last year. We could also add another Mercosur associate state like Colombia with 3 interceptions this year for 1 last year. South Africa is down in the number of interceptions, but it must be taken into account that the bulk of its exports began in September and that it has suspended exports of oranges from its areas with pests, although not from the others . Despite suspending these shipments, it still accumulates 16 interceptions so far this year, 4 of them in September, "which shows once again that it does not comply with its commitments". Of these 16 interceptions, 13 correspond to Phyllosticta citricarpa, a fungus that causes the dreaded disease of citrus black spot. In this sense, LA UNIÓ is calling for an increase in requirements and controls at origin, as detections for this fungus are skyrocketing. For all these circumstances and the danger they entail, THE UNION requests the automatic suspension of imports from those third countries that cannot guarantee the phytosanitary safety of their shipments because it is always the same countries that lead the list of interceptions. In the same way, the organization calls for the implementation of a unified cold system for all imports and countries and for the community authorities to have a single standardized criterion for all. On the other hand, THE UNION believes that ratifying or signing agreements by the EU such as with Mercosur or South Africa is a serious mistake, because there are no deep or rigorous impact studies on the effects they cause in our agricultural sector nor about the consequences of the possible entry of a pest, such as those we are already paying with the Xylella or the South African Cotton. Carles Peris, general secretary of LA UNIÓ, points out that "everything that is agreements without reciprocity in production standards or in the use of active materials, without full phytosanitary guarantees in terms of pests, without acquiring the commitments of reduction proposed by the European strategy From farm to table and without ordering in terms of import periods so that they do not overlap with European productions that are not in deficit, is to go back to making the same mistakes as always that always cause a huge impact negative in the European sector". Peris also indicates that "with such an exorbitant increase in production costs, it is necessary to take all matters related to the protection of European plant health more seriously, because the introduction of a new pest or disease would further increase the costs of these treatments and would also devalue the role of citrus fruits as suppliers of fruit aimed at the fresh market".
