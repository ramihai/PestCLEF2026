International: A new Cavendish variety with complete resistance to Panama disease R4T will be available in 2024 A new variety of Cavendish banana with complete resistance to Panama disease R4T will be commercially available from the beginning of 2024. The Israeli company Rahan Meristem, with the help of its Colombian partner Luis Alberto Restrepo, president of Banarica Colombia, has developed Cavendish banana plants with complete resistance to R4T Panama disease. Other improvements of the selected Cavendish cultivar are increased fruit production, early flowering and improved plant architecture. After many years of scientific work and trials in Israel, the Netherlands and field trials in the Philippines, they achieved their goal of saving the well-known Cavendish banana. The technology does not imply the use of genetic engineering. Tropical race 4 Panama disease (T4R) spreads rapidly and causes serious damage to plants, causing significant losses to banana growers around the world. So far, some banana cultivars with some resistance to TR4 have been released. The most effective strategy has been to apply containment and biosecurity measures to stop the advance of the disease. Ron Diner, CEO of Rahan, explains: “The R4T strain of Panama disease is the main focus of research in the banana industry. The fact that our technology can benefit the banana sector in several ways is a positive step towards its ultimate commercialization. We are now working with our partners in Rahan to save the Cavendish banana.” Developing the new Cavendish variety, Diner says, took more than seven years of scientific work that included field testing of the new variety, which proved fully resistant to Fusarium (R4T). Rahan started with random mutations in banana plants from tissue culture in Israel. They then propagated a new line of more than 10,000 Cavendish varieties. These varieties were then brought to the greenhouse experiments at Wageningen University in the Netherlands and tested for their susceptibility and resistance to TR4. Field experiments were done in the Philippines with clones selected from infected fields. In 2022, the Fusiarium resistant banana variety was tested, genomically sequenced and confirmed. “The best of the Philippines are already in Colombia in quarantine. Afterwards, they will be planted in T4R-infected areas in that country in 2023," concludes Diner. Its commercial availability is scheduled for 2024.
