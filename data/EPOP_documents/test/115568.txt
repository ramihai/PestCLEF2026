Studies on weed hosts of tomato brown rugose fruit virus
Tomato brown rugose fruit virus (Tobamovirus, ToBRFV – EPPO A2 quarantine pest) was first described in tomatoes grown in greenhouses in the Jordan Valley in 2015 (Salem et al., 2016). Since then, this virus has been detected every year in tomato-growing areas across Jordan, and these continued outbreaks have raised concerns about the possible role of weed hosts in the epidemiology of the disease. From February 2019 to November 2021, weeds were collected from tomato fields and greenhouses in the Jordan Valley and the highlands of the main tomato growing areas. A wide range of weed species (30 plant species corresponding to 16 families) was found in tomato crops, and most plants collected were symptomless (with a few exceptions showing yellowing, stunting, mosaic or marbling). Out of 258 samples collected, ToBRFV was detected (DAS-ELISA followed by mechanical inoculation with Nicotiana tabacum, D. metel, D. stramonium and confirmed by RT-PCR) in 114 samples corresponding to 12 plant species: Amaranthus retroflexus, Beta vulgaris subsp. maritima and Chenopodium mural (Amaranthaceae); Conyza canadensis and Taraxacum officinale (Asteraceae); Malva parviflora (Malvaceae); Oxalis corniculata (Oxalidaceae); Portulaca oleracea (Portulaceae); Veronica syriaca (Scrophulariaceae); Solanum elaeagnifolium and S. nigrum (Solanaceae); and Corchorus olitorius (Tiliaceae). Plant species for which more than 50% of the samples collected were infected included A. retroflexus, C. canadensis, T. officinale, C. murale, M. parviflora, V. syriaca and S. nigrum. During these studies, mature fruits of S. nigrum were also collected from symptomatic plants for further experiments to assess seed transmission of ToBRFV. The results showed that ToBRFV could be transmitted from S. nigrum contaminated seeds to seedlings at a low rate (about 1.9%); and as a control, all seedlings from healthy seeds were confirmed virus-free by DAS-ELISA and RT-PCR. The authors concluded that management strategies for ToBRFV in tomato crops should be reconsidered to include appropriate weed control. For more information:EPPOwww.gd.eppo.int
